02

The seven democratic function categories

Category Definition
Civic participation & deliberation

This category includes organizations that expand civic engagement, voter participation, and structured public deliberation — from get-out-the-vote campaigns to digital democracy tools to civic education programs. It excludes organizations focused primarily on election administration or the legal mechanics of voting, which fall under Election integrity.
Election integrity

This category covers organizations working to ensure elections are free, fair, and trustworthy — including secure voting systems, transparent administration, legal protection of voting rights, and election auditing. It is distinct from civic participation in that it focuses on the integrity of the electoral process itself rather than who participates in it.
Accountability & transparency

This category includes organizations that hold government and powerful institutions accountable — through investigative journalism, litigation, open-data tools, public records advocacy, and anti-corruption work. Organizations are included here when their core function is oversight and accountability rather than advocacy for a specific policy position.
Information environment & epistemics

This category covers organizations working on the health of the information ecosystem — including misinformation research, media literacy, fact-checking, platform governance, and the study of how information spreads. As AI reshapes content production and distribution, this pillar has become increasingly central to democratic resilience. It excludes journalism organizations unless their primary focus is the information environment itself.
Civil society capacity

This category includes organizations that fund, train, or build infrastructure for civil society — philanthropies, capacity-building intermediaries, civic technology developers, and nonprofit support organizations. These are organizations whose primary contribution is enabling other democratic actors rather than doing front-line advocacy or research themselves.
Democratic resilience monitoring

This category covers organizations that measure and track democratic health — producing indices of backsliding, monitoring polarization, and maintaining the empirical record of democratic change over time. It is distinct from accountability work in that its primary output is measurement and analysis rather than direct intervention. Organizations in this category tend to serve researchers and policymakers rather than the general public.
Governance & policy

This category includes organizations that shape the rules, norms, and frameworks governing AI and democratic institutions — through policy advocacy, standards development, regulatory engagement, and legal reform. It is the broadest category and includes both technology-focused organizations working on AI governance and democracy-focused organizations engaging with technology policy.

03

AI use codes

Tool
The organization uses AI as a tool in its own work — for example, using machine learning to analyze data, detect misinformation, or automate research tasks. AI is an input to their operations, not the subject of their advocacy or research.
Subject
The organization works on AI as a policy, research, or advocacy topic — for example, researching AI's effects on elections, advocating for AI regulation, or tracking how AI is being deployed by government. They study or shape AI; they don't necessarily use it themselves.
Both
The organization both uses AI in its own work and works on AI as a subject — for example, a research lab that builds AI-powered tools and also publishes policy recommendations on AI governance.
Neither
The organization's work is relevant to democratic resilience but does not have a meaningful relationship to AI — either as a tool they use or as a subject they engage with. Included because the landscape aims to map the full field, not only AI-native actors.

04

Limitations and caveats

Placeholder paragraph one. All categorizations in this landscape reflect the author's judgment and should be read as interpretive rather than definitive. Reasonable people may categorize the same organization differently — particularly for organizations whose work spans multiple democratic functions. Where an organization's work clearly spans two pillars, it was assigned to the category that best reflects its primary focus; a future version of this landscape may allow multi-category tagging.

Placeholder paragraph two. This landscape is U.S.-focused by design, but the boundary is imprecise. Some organizations included here do significant international work; others are U.S.-based but operate globally. Organizations were included if their primary base of operations or primary focus is the United States. The landscape is also not exhaustive — particularly in areas like civic technology and local civic infrastructure, where the field is large and fragmented.
