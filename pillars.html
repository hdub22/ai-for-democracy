Core concepts and focus areas

Terms

Understanding democratic resilience

The project uses the following foundational definitions.

Term Definition
Democracy A system of government in which power is held by the people, exercised through free and fair elections, protected by the rule of law, and constrained by rights that no majority can simply vote away.
Democratic resilience The capacity of democratic institutions, norms, and civil society to absorb pressure, resist authoritarian capture, and recover from shocks — whether from internal polarization, disinformation, foreign interference, or technological disruption.
Democratic backsliding The gradual erosion of democratic norms and institutions — often through legal means — by actors seeking to concentrate power, undermine checks and balances, or hollow out the mechanisms that hold governments accountable.

Focus areas

Categorizing organizations’ work

Each organization in the dataset is categorized by its predominant work within one or more of the following focus areas.

🗳 Civic participation & deliberation →

Healthy democracies depend on active, informed citizens who can meaningfully engage in public decisions. This focus area covers organizations that expand voter participation, support civic education, and create structured spaces for public deliberation. From get-out-the-vote efforts to digital democracy tools, this work sustains the connective tissue between citizens and government.

Organizations in this space

  • National Conference on Citizenship
  • Civic Nation
  • Democracy Works
🛡 Election integrity →

Free and fair elections are the bedrock of democratic legitimacy. This focus area includes organizations that safeguard the mechanics of elections — from secure voting systems and transparent administration to legal protection of voting rights. Their work spans technology, law, and policy to ensure elections remain trustworthy and verifiable.

Organizations in this space

  • Brennan Center for Justice
  • Verified Voting
  • Common Cause
🔎 Accountability & transparency →

Democratic power requires oversight. This focus area covers organizations that monitor government conduct, expose corruption, and ensure public institutions remain answerable to the people they serve. Their tools range from investigative journalism and litigation to open-data infrastructure and public records advocacy.

Organizations in this space

  • Protect Democracy
  • Demos
  • OpenSecrets
📡 Information environment & epistemics →

Democracy depends on citizens being able to form accurate beliefs about the world. This focus area covers organizations working on misinformation, media literacy, platform governance, and the health of the broader information ecosystem. As AI reshapes how content is produced and distributed, this space has become increasingly central to democratic resilience.

Organizations in this space

  • First Draft
  • Poynter Institute
  • News Literacy Project
🌱 Civil society capacity →

A resilient democracy needs a robust ecosystem of independent organizations that can organize, advocate, and serve communities. This focus area covers groups that fund, train, and support civil society — from community organizers to nonprofit infrastructure builders to civic technology developers. Capacity in this space is what allows all the other focus areas to function.

Organizations in this space

  • Knight Foundation
  • Omidyar Network
  • Civic Hall
⚖️ Governance & policy →

The rules and norms governing AI and democratic institutions are still being written. This focus area covers organizations that shape policy, develop standards, and advocate for governance frameworks that protect democratic values in the face of technological change. Their work sits at the intersection of law, technology, and political power.

Organizations in this space

  • Center for Democracy & Technology
  • Partnership on AI
  • AI Now Institute
